The Invisible Crisis

What Is Light Pollution?

Light pollution is the excessive, misdirected, or obstructed use of artificial light at night. Unlike most pollution, it is invisible in daylight — yet it operates continuously, disrupting natural dark cycles for every living organism on Earth.

Singapore's 24-hour city culture produces a permanent orange haze above our island. Most Singaporeans under 30 have never seen the Milky Way from home.

The Four Types

  • Skyglow — the bright halo over cities
  • Glare — excessive brightness causing discomfort
  • Light Trespass — unwanted light entering your bedroom
  • Clutter — confusing groups of competing light sources

Why It Matters

Who Does Light Pollution Hurt?

The consequences go far beyond “not seeing stars.” Light pollution actively harms wildlife, ecosystems, and human health — every single night.

Migratory Birds

Singapore lies along the East Asian–Australasian Flyway, one of the world's major migratory bird routes. Excessive lighting can disorient migrating birds, causing collisions with buildings and disrupting navigation during nighttime migration. Singapore’s NSS Bird Group confirmed this threat in 2022, noting birds circling bright areas until exhausted.

Critical Impact

Urban Biodiversity

Artificial lighting in parks and streets attracts nocturnal insects away from their natural habitats, disrupting feeding, breeding, and pollination cycles.

Critical Impact

Human Health

Artificial light suppresses melatonin production. A 2021 review in the British Journal of Cancer (Jones, NIH/NCI) confirmed circadian disruption from artificial light at night is linked to increased cancer risk.

Real-World Evidence

Three Stories that Changed Everything

Around the world — and right here in our region — light pollution is destroying what took millions of years to evolve.

📍 Sisters’ Islands Marine Park, Singapore

Coral Reefs Losing the Moon

The Selangor River was once home to one of Southeast Asia’s most spectacular firefly displays. As riverside resort lighting expanded from 2000–2010, firefly populations dropped by an estimated 70%. The disrupted light cycles broke their mating signals.

📍 Pulau Ubin, Singapore

Our Last Relatively Dark Sky

Pulau Ubin — just 10 km from Changi — hosts rare nocturnal wildlife including the Sunda pangolin and bat species dependent on dark foraging corridors. The creeping amber glow is now measurable from the island’s eastern shore.

The International Dark-Sky Association

The IDA’s Dark Sky Places Programme has certified over 200 reserves worldwide. Switching to shielded, warm-spectrum LEDs reduced skyglow measurably within one year, cutting energy bills by 20–50%. Source: DarkSky International & NPS, 2023
